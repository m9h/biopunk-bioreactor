Technical Note · Errata + Comparison

OpenEvo Corrections & the Open-Bioreactor Matrix

A fair-minded correction of the errors and overstatements in the OpenEvo preprint, followed by a like-for-like matrix of six open continuous-culture platforms — so the trade-offs are legible without re-reading six papers.

2026-07-10 Subject: bioRxiv 2026.07.06.735356 + field Not peer review; a reader's audit
First, credit where due

OpenEvo is a real contribution and the corrections below are not a dismissal of it. Its genuine strengths: a peak-to-peak growth readout tied to hardware dilution events, which sidesteps local-maxima noise; a 940 nm OD path that avoids optogenetic and ambient overlap, with an inverse model holding R² ≥ 0.98 out to OD600 6.0; a closed autoclavable vessel with better sterility than Chi.Bio's open tube; a 4,000-hour remote run demonstrating real robustness; and an illustrated assembly manual validated by having an inexperienced undergraduate build a working unit — a reproducibility contribution rarer than it should be. It is the cheapest buildable three-media selection cycler in the field. The issues below are correctable and mostly concern framing and analysis, not the hardware.

01 Corrections to the OpenEvo preprint

Grouped by kind: verifiable errors, framing / omissions, and methodological limitations. Severity is this reader's judgement of impact on the paper's claims.

Error 1 · Internal contradictionHigh

The headline growth-rate figure disagrees with itself.

Abstract: "Evolved cells grew 55% faster than wild-type at 12% salt."
Results: "a doubling time of 4.9 hours versus 7.6 hours (a 36% increase in growth rate)."

Both describe the same data, and the two numbers are two different quantities. A doubling time of 7.6 → 4.9 h is a 36% reduction in doubling time but a 55% increase in growth rate (rate ∝ 1/doubling time; 1/4.9 ÷ 1/7.6 = 1.55). The abstract's 55% is the correct growth-rate figure. The results sentence mislabels the 36% doubling-time reduction as a growth-rate increase — so the results text is internally wrong, and a reader cannot tell which claim to quote. Fix: state both explicitly — "36% shorter doubling time, equivalently a 55% higher growth rate."

Error 2 · False claim about prior artHigh

Pioreactor's hardware is open source; the paper says it is not.

"A research-scale device called the 'Pioreactor' is available for purchase, but only the software is open source."

A public Pioreactor/hardware repository exists, and the Pioreactor hardware is released under CC BY-SA 4.0 (per its LICENSE file) — an openly licensed hardware repo. The claim is used to clear competitive space for OpenEvo's novelty, and it is false. Fix: describe Pioreactor accurately as open-hardware-and-software, and distinguish it on the real axis — it is a supported product with a fleet platform, not a from-scratch build.

Framing 1 · Nearest prior art uncitedMedium

Chi.Bio is never cited, and it is the closest comparator that exists.

Chi.Bio (PLOS Biology 2020) is open hardware and software, roughly the same ~$300, does in-situ measurement and continuous culture, and predates OpenEvo by six years. The preprint surveys eVOLVER, the Toprak morbidostat, and Pioreactor, then frames an unfilled gap — while omitting the one system that most directly occupies it. The omission makes the gap look larger than it is. Fix: cite and contrast Chi.Bio; the honest distinction is selection fluidics and buildability, not existence of a gap.

Framing 2 · Unsupported superlativeMedium

"The most affordable modern continuous growth system available" is not established.

Pioreactor ships turnkey at $329 with no build, and EVE builds a triplicate system for $115–200. Against those, an unqualified affordability superlative does not hold. Fix: qualify — e.g., "among the lowest-cost buildable systems with three-media selection," which is defensible.

Framing 3 · Success overstatedMedium

The evolution campaign includes a hard failure the framing understates.

The 9% salt step did not partially adapt — growth ceased, OD flatlined, and the run terminated at cycle 152. The adaptation claim rests entirely on a single hand-picked glycerol stock at cycle 145, revived and assayed offline. The result is real and worth reporting; the framing should foreground that it is one successful selection step with a failed extension, not a ladder.

Method 1 · No replicatesHigh

n = 1 forecloses causal attribution of the mutations.

A single clone from a single population was sequenced. Without independent replicate lineages, there is no convergence signal — no way to distinguish a selected driver from a hitchhiker or a neutral passenger. The reported SNPs and two large deletions therefore cannot be attributed to the low-salt adaptation. This is the field's central inference requirement (the LTEE runs twelve populations for exactly this reason), and it is the most consequential limitation here. Fix: replicate populations, and identify mutations recurring across them.

Method 2 · Wrong variant callerMedium

Structural variants were called with a default read mapper, not breseq.

"…mapped to the DS2 genome using Geneious's default mapper with low-medium sensitivity for 5 iterations."

The key finding — a ~110 kb pHV4 deletion with "sparse read mapping … primarily transposon-associated elements" — is IS-mediated structural rearrangement, precisely the case a default short-read mapper handles worst. breseq exists for microbial-evolution resequencing including IS mobilisation, and its author, Jeffrey Barrick, is cited five times in this very paper. Fix: re-call with breseq; the deletion boundaries and IS junctions need it, and long-read confirmation (which the authors already flag) would settle the pHV4 structure.

Method 3 · No systematic archivingLow

One manual glycerol stock is not a fossil record.

The paper cites Barrick 2023 — "Daily Transfers, Archiving Populations, and Measuring Fitness" — as motivation, then implements neither archiving nor fitness measurement in the automated system. Head-to-head ancestor-vs-descendant competition, the gold standard the cited work describes, is impossible without a time-series archive. Fix: even manual periodic stocks at fixed cycle intervals would materially strengthen future runs.

04 Independent use — the credibility signal

For a platform with no methods paper, third-party use is the only external validation. The picture is uneven, and it tracks each platform's age and purpose.

  • Chi.Bio — integrated by external tooling such as ReacSight (Nat Commun 2022) for bioreactor-array control, and cited across optogenetic feedback work. Established.
  • eVOLVER — the strongest independent record: continuous-evolution systems including ePACE, mammalian long-term evolution, and evolved base editors are built on it by groups other than Khalil's.
  • Toprak morbidostat — a decade of adaptation; the reference design that later devices (including EVE and OpenEvo) position themselves against.
  • Pioreactor — broad hobbyist, classroom, and workshop adoption (e.g. CSM 2025), but no indexed peer-reviewed methods citations. Its credibility is commercial and community, not bibliographic — a real consideration if you need to cite your instrument.
  • EVE — primarily educational uptake to date; younger and narrower than eVOLVER.
  • OpenEvo — days old at this writing; no independent use yet. Too new to judge.

05 Bottom line, by what you actually need

  • Characterise a strain or circuit (reporters, physiology, feedback): Chi.Bio, decisively — nothing else has the in-situ optics.
  • Run reliable parallel cultures now, minimal fuss: Pioreactor if turnkey and supported matters; eVOLVER if you need 16 vials of arbitrary per-vial control and have a machine shop.
  • Evolve on a teaching or shoestring budget: EVE — cheapest replicate-capable, full docs.
  • Antibiotic-resistance / drug-gradient evolution: a morbidostat lineage device.
  • Three-media selection cycling in one cheap closed vessel: OpenEvo — its real niche, once §1's analysis gaps are addressed.
  • Rigorous ALE that produces evidence: none as shipped. It needs replicates + archiving + breseq — the argument of the companion build proposal.
