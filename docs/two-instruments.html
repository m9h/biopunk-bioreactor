Instrument Proposal · Open Hardware

Two Instruments, One Ecosystem

Chi.Bio and OpenEvo serve different purposes, so the right move is to build the best machine for each purpose first — and only then ask whether one machine can do both. This is that argument, in three parts. The short answer: build two, share the parts.

Rev. B · 2026-07-10 Sources: PLOS Biol 18:e3000794 · bioRxiv 2026.07.06.735356 · Pioreactor / eVOLVER / EVE Status: pre-decisional
The two purposes, stated cleanly

Purpose 1 — Characterisation. Hold a culture in a defined, unchanging state and measure it richly, in situ, in real time, while closing feedback loops around it. You want signal density per reactor and a handful of reactors running different conditions in parallel. This is Chi.Bio's purpose.

Purpose 2 — Evolution. Push a culture through selection regimes unattended for weeks, and produce evidence that adaptation occurred and which mutation caused it. You want cheap chambers in numbers, archiving, and long-run robustness. This is OpenEvo's purpose.

These purposes pull hardware in opposite directions. A machine optimised for one is actively worse at the other — which is the whole reason Part 3 is a real question and not a foregone conclusion.

Part 1 · Purpose: Characterisation

The best in-situ characterisation instrument

Start from Chi.Bio, because its architecture is already correct for this purpose. The upgrades sharpen what it is; none of them compromise the one property that makes it work.

Design law: non-contact is inviolable. Every Chi.Bio subsystem but the heat plate is non-contact, which is why tubes hot-swap and sterilisation is trivial. Any sensor added to this machine must read through the vessel wall — optical, not immersed. The moment you put an electrode in the culture, you have built the other machine.

What to keep, unchanged

  • The non-contact discipline — the crown jewel, and the constraint every addition below respects.
  • PID + model-predictive control — OD held within ~2% of setpoint, temperature to a defined path, plus the dithering mode for growth-rate estimation at near-constant density.
  • Eight reactors, one control computer — for characterisation, parallelism means eight richly instrumented reactors running eight conditions. This is the right kind of parallelism for this purpose (contrast Part 2).
  • Chemostat / turbidostat flexibility — same hardware, either mode. Pin growth rate to study physiology, or pin density to study rate.

Part 2 · Purpose: Evolution

The best automated evolution machine

Start from OpenEvo, because its virtues — cheap, closed, buildable, standalone — are exactly right for long unattended runs. But OpenEvo as published cannot produce the evidence an evolution experiment exists to produce. Fixing that, not adding sensors, is the work.

Design law: cheap × many, and resist sensing bloat. The output of this machine is not a rich time series — it is an archived, sequenced, replicated record of adaptation. Every dollar spent instrumenting a single chamber is a dollar not spent on the second chamber. Loading it with characterisation sensing does not make it a better evolution machine; it makes it a worse one, and invites the category error of treating a $300 turbidostat as a bioprocess reactor.

The two things OpenEvo is missing, and neither is a sensor

Fix I · Replicate populations

n = 1 is not a throughput limit. It is a failure of causal inference.

The LTEE runs twelve populations because you cannot separate a driver mutation from a hitchhiker in a single lineage — convergence across independent replicates is the only evidence you have that a mutation was selected rather than merely present. OpenEvo sequenced one clone from one population, so its SNPs and two deletions carry no such evidence; nobody, including the authors, can say which one did the work.

The build: not one richly-instrumented reactor, but eight cheap chambers under one coordinating interface — OpenEvo's own suggested workaround, made first-class. This is the opposite of Part 1's parallelism: minimal instrumentation per chamber, many chambers. It is also why the two machines do not share a reactor design even though both are "eight-way parallel."

Fix II · Archiving

The LTEE's real innovation was the freezer, not the flask.

A sample to −80 °C every 500 generations means any ancestor can be revived and raced head-to-head against any descendant in the same medium — which is what makes fitness measured rather than inferred. OpenEvo took one glycerol stock, by hand, at cycle 145. That single stock is the entire evidentiary basis for its adaptation claim.

Sharper still: OpenEvo cites Barrick 2023 — "Daily Transfers, Archiving Populations, and Measuring Fitness" as its proof that manual passaging is the bottleneck. The paper it cites as the problem contains the two methods it omitted.

The build: an automated chilled fraction collector on the existing waste line. It already pumps culture out on every dilution; divert a programmable fraction, on a generation-count schedule, into a chilled glycerol archive. As far as I can find, nobody has built this, and it is the single highest-value addition to an evolution machine.

The trap the ALE literature exists to prevent

ALE selects for growth, and making product costs growth. Run naive selection on a production strain and you build a machine that finds the mutant which deleted your pathway — fast. The field's answer is growth-coupled design first: engineer a strain where product formation is stoichiometrically obligatory for growth, then select. Read in that light, OpenEvo's negative-selection media line is a hardware stab at the counterselection that growth-coupling achieves in metabolism. Right instinct; build on it.

Part 3 · The actual question

Should you combine them into one machine?

Mostly no. Build two instruments, and share the ecosystem beneath them. Here is the reasoning, not just the verdict.

The two optimal machines from Parts 1 and 2 diverge on every axis that matters. A single machine forced to satisfy both columns is dominated by each specialist at its own job.

AxisCharacterisation wantsEvolution wants
Reactor economicsFew, richly instrumentedMany, minimally instrumented
Culture contactNon-contact, inviolableImmersed electrodes fine
Culture stateHeld at defined steady stateFree to run to stationary phase
Run lengthHours to daysWeeks to months, unattended
Primary outputDense real-time signalArchived, sequenced, replicated record
Sensing philosophyMore is betterLess is better

Notice the tension resolves itself cleanly rather than requiring compromise: the sensor modalities sort by contact. Optode DO/pH and dispersive spectrometry are optical and non-contact, so they land on the characterisation machine. Conductivity, ERT, and β-dispersion viable-cell-density all need immersed electrodes, so they land on the evolution machine — whose closed autoclavable vessel welcomes them. There is almost no sensor that both machines want and only one can have. That is the strongest evidence that these are two instruments, not one.

What the two machines do share — the ecosystem

Combining is right at the level of the platform, wrong at the level of the product. Four shared layers give you most of the benefit of a merge with none of the compromise:

  • A module footprint. Adopt IO Rodeo's 25.4 × 20 mm / dual-JST-SH standard for both. Both papers gesture at modularity — OpenEvo's "magnetic bay", Chi.Bio's "expansion port" — and neither defines a footprint. One standard, one parts catalogue, both machines.
  • The sensor module designs. A DO optode reader is the same board whether it reads a characterisation vessel through the wall or gets borrowed for an aerobic-selection run. Design each sensor once; deploy on either chassis.
  • The firmware / interface framework. One control-and-logging codebase, two hardware profiles. OpenEvo's remote-access GUI and Chi.Bio's custom-program Python framework are converging on the same thing already.
  • The calibration reference. One open-source colorimeter on the bench calibrates the OD path of both machines. Shared, not embedded.
The one honest exception

If you only ever intend to do ALE with fitness measurement and never pure characterisation, there is a single-machine case: the evolution machine from Part 2, built on eight cheap chambers with archiving, is genuinely close to complete, and bolting the characterisation sensing onto it would be waste. The merge tempts only in the reverse direction — a characterisation lab that occasionally evolves a strain — and even there, a second cheap chamber is less money and less compromise than making every reactor do both. When in doubt: two machines, shared parts.

Part 4 · The wider landscape

Where Pioreactor and the others fit — and the third purpose they reveal

Two purposes turn out to be an undercount. Pioreactor is best-of-breed, but for neither of them — it owns a third axis that Chi.Bio and OpenEvo, both build-it-yourself research artifacts, never optimise for: reliability and fleet operation as a product.

Purpose 3 — Scale-out operation. Run N reliable reactors now, with minimal build effort, a maintained software platform, cluster management, and support. The value is not signal density (Purpose 1) or archived evidence (Purpose 2) — it is reproducibility, uptime, and not having to build anything. This axis is orthogonal to the other two.

The uncomfortable implication for the evolution machine

Part 2's fix for OpenEvo's n = 1 problem — "eight cheap chambers under one coordinating interface" — is a description of Pioreactor's leader/worker cluster, already productised. So the best P2 machine may be Pioreactor + an archiving plugin + a batch-excursion automation, not a scaled-up OpenEvo. OpenEvo's genuine remaining edge shrinks to two things Pioreactor lacks: the three-media selection fluidics and the closed autoclavable vessel. Both are addable to a Pioreactor; the cluster, the platform, and the plugin ecosystem are not easily addable to an OpenEvo.

Correction to the record: OpenEvo's introduction states Pioreactor is "available for purchase, but only the software is open source." A public Pioreactor/hardware repository exists and the hardware is under CC BY-SA 4.0 (per its LICENSE file) — openly licensed. The dismissal is not accurate, and it is the same paper that never cites Chi.Bio and under-uses breseq. Weigh its competitive framing accordingly.

05 Build order, two tracks

Shared foundation first, then the tracks diverge. C = characterisation, E = evolution.

S0

Shared foundation

Build one stock Chi.Bio and one stock OpenEvo. Adopt the module footprint and a common firmware framework. Stand up the shared calibration reference and cross-calibrate the 650 nm and 940 nm OD paths on one culture across OD600 0.2–6.0. Nothing forks until both baselines agree.

C1

Characterisation · optode DO + pH

Non-contact fluorescence spots read through the wall. The first real physiology upgrade, and it proves the shared sensor-module path.

C2

Characterisation · dispersive spectrometer + atmosphere

C12880MA alongside the AS7341, and a sealed gas-controlled lid. This is now the best in-situ characterisation instrument on the page.

E1

Evolution · archive + batch excursion + conductivity

The two findings plus the media-verification sensor. All firmware-and-cheap-hardware; this is what turns OpenEvo's output into evidence.

E2

Evolution · eight-chamber replicate array + breseq

Replicates under one interface, and the variant-calling pipeline that makes convergence legible. This is now the best automated evolution machine on the page.

E3

Evolution · ERT, then viable-cell-density (research)

Spectra for mixing/biofilm at 80 kHz, gated to the settle window; then β-dispersion viable biomass, gated entirely on Q1.

06 Open questions for Jean Rintoul

These bear on the evolution machine's E3 research tier — mixing verification and viable-cell-density. Q1 is load-bearing; nothing else on the page depends on it.

  1. Is Spectra's 80 kHz ceiling silicon or firmware? AN-1271 specs the ADuCM350's DFT impedance excitation to 200 kHz, with 80 kHz being the HSDACCON low-power/high-power crossover — which reads as configuration, not hardware. Was 80 kHz a power, noise, thermal, or patient-safety call? Does firmware expose high-power mode, and if pushed to 200 kHz, what degrades first — SNR, DFT settling, or excitation-DAC flatness? The β-dispersion starts at exactly 100 kHz, so this bit is the difference between "off the curve" and "on its lower foot" — and viable-cell-density on yeast (fc ≈ 2–3 MHz) becomes real if it clears.
  2. Did the 32-electrode Spectra land true tetrapolar drive? The EIT_EE README notes the 8-electrode board was not tetrapolar and that tetrapolar differential was intended. Is "differential referencing" narrower than that? Does it follow AN-1302's 4-wire bioisolated config? We would measure into archaeal media at 12–18% w/v salt, where electrode polarisation is brutal at the low end of the band.
  3. Has Spectra ever imaged a vessel rather than a body? A ~2.5 cm glass tube of brine is not a thorax; GREIT's priors assume a thorax-shaped domain and physiological conductivity. Does reconstruction hold at that geometry, and is 32 electrodes around that circumference over- or under-determined?

07 Risks, and what this is not

  • Electrodes cost the evolution machine its hot-swap. Immersed electrodes foul, polarise, drift, and need autoclaving. A flex circuit laminated to the outside of the glass — capacitively coupled, lossy — preserves hot-swap; the 8_electrode_flex directory in the OpenEIT repo suggests this is not novel.
  • Wall biofilm is both signal and noise for ERT. EIT's boundary sensitivity is highest exactly where biofilm grows — the reason it can detect biofilm is the reason biofilm corrupts the conductivity reading you wanted for mixing. Separating them is not obvious.
  • A mutagenesis controller can hill-climb into meltdown. Both papers float UV-driven feedback mutagenesis. Six of twelve LTEE populations became hypermutators via mismatch-repair defects, and some later re-lowered their mutation rate. A controller that cranks mutagenesis whenever adaptation stalls needs a model, not a threshold.
  • Neither machine, however upgraded, is a bioprocess instrument. Both stay ~20 mL benchtop research devices — no gas sparging at scale, no fed-batch, no CIP/SIP. They live at the discovery end; the strain then goes to a real instrumented bioreactor. The evolution machine's added sensing makes the category error easier, not harder — which is the deeper reason Part 2 says resist.

Two flags for its authors: (1) the abstract's "55% faster" and the results' "36% increase in growth rate" describe the same cycle-145 data — 36% is the doubling-time reduction, 55% the rate increase; the results text mislabels one.
